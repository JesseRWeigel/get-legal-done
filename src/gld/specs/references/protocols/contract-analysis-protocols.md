# Contract Analysis Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for contract law analysis.

## Protocol: Contract Formation Analysis

### When to Use
Determining whether a valid and enforceable contract exists between parties.

### Steps
1. **Identify the offer** — was there a manifestation of present intent to be bound, communicated to the offeree, with definite and certain terms?
2. **Distinguish from preliminary negotiations** — advertisements, price quotes, and invitations to deal are generally not offers (exception: ads with specific terms and quantity limits, per Lefkowitz)
3. **Identify the acceptance** — was there an unequivocal assent to the terms of the offer by the offeree? Check the method of acceptance (promise, performance, or silence in limited cases)
4. **Apply the mailbox rule** — acceptance is effective upon dispatch (if authorized medium); rejection and revocation are effective upon receipt
5. **Check for consideration** — was there a bargained-for exchange of legal detriment or benefit? Watch for past consideration (not valid), illusory promises, and pre-existing duty rule
6. **For UCC Article 2 (sale of goods)**: apply the merchant's firm offer rule (§ 2-205), battle of the forms (§ 2-207), and gap-filler provisions (price, delivery, payment terms)
7. **Check defenses to formation** — statute of frauds, incapacity, illegality, duress, undue influence, misrepresentation, unconscionability, mistake (mutual vs unilateral)
8. **State the conclusion** — a valid contract was/was not formed, identifying any defenses that might void or voidify it

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying UCC Article 2 to service contracts or real estate transactions (UCC Art. 2 applies only to sale of goods)
- Applying the common law mirror image rule under UCC § 2-207 (UCC allows acceptance with additional/different terms)
- Treating moral obligation as valid consideration
- Forgetting to analyze the statute of frauds for contracts that fall within its scope (goods ≥ $500, real property, suretyship, contracts not performable within one year, marriage)

## Protocol: Contract Interpretation

### When to Use
Determining the meaning of contract terms when parties dispute the meaning of provisions.

### Steps
1. **Start with the plain meaning** — apply the ordinary meaning of the words used in the contract
2. **Apply the four corners rule** (majority approach) — if the contract is unambiguous on its face, extrinsic evidence is inadmissible to contradict or vary the terms
3. **Check for ambiguity** — is the term reasonably susceptible to more than one interpretation? (Patent ambiguity = obvious; latent ambiguity = revealed by extrinsic facts)
4. **If ambiguous, apply the parol evidence rule** — prior or contemporaneous agreements are inadmissible to contradict an integrated writing, but may be admitted to supplement a partially integrated agreement or to explain ambiguity
5. **Apply canons of construction** — expressio unius, ejusdem generis, noscitur a sociis, contra proferentem (construe against the drafter), specific terms control general terms
6. **Consider course of performance, course of dealing, and trade usage** (UCC § 1-303) — these supplement and explain written terms
7. **Apply the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** — every contract includes this implied term; it prevents one party from destroying the other's right to receive contract benefits
8. **State the most reasonable interpretation** — the interpretation that gives effect to all provisions and avoids absurd or unreasonable results

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Admitting parol evidence to contradict a fully integrated agreement
- Applying contra proferentem when the contract is unambiguous (it applies only to ambiguous terms)
- Confusing the parol evidence rule with the statute of frauds (different doctrines)
- Ignoring trade usage that may give specialized meaning to ordinary terms

## Protocol: Breach and Remedies Analysis

### When to Use
Evaluating whether a breach has occurred and determining available remedies.

### Steps
1. **Identify the breached obligation** — which specific contractual duty was not performed?
2. **Classify the breach** — material breach (excuses the non-breaching party's performance and permits termination) or minor breach (only permits damages, not termination)
3. **Apply the materiality factors** (Restatement § 241) — extent of deprivation, adequacy of compensation, extent of part performance, likelihood of cure, good faith
4. **Check for anticipatory repudiation** — did a party unequivocally indicate inability or unwillingness to perform before performance was due? (UCC § 2-610; Hochster v. De La Tour)
5. **Determine expectation damages** — place the non-breaching party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed (benefit of the bargain)
6. **Apply limitations on damages** — foreseeability (Hadley v. Baxendale), certainty (damages must be proven with reasonable certainty), mitigation (non-breaching party must take reasonable steps to avoid unnecessary loss)
7. **Consider alternative remedies** — reliance damages, restitution, specific performance (when legal remedy is inadequate, e.g., unique goods, real property), liquidated damages (if reasonable forecast and not a penalty)
8. **For UCC**: apply the buyer's remedies (§§ 2-711–2-717: cover, market price differential, incidental/consequential damages) or seller's remedies (§§ 2-703–2-710: resale, market price, lost profit)

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Granting specific performance for ordinary goods when money damages are adequate
- Confusing expectation, reliance, and restitution damage measures
- Enforcing liquidated damages clauses that are actually penalties (amount must be reasonable relative to anticipated or actual loss)
- Ignoring the duty to mitigate damages

## Protocol: UCC vs Common Law Distinction

### When to Use
Determining which body of law governs a transaction.

### Steps
1. **Identify the subject matter** — is the transaction primarily for the sale of goods (UCC Art. 2) or services/real property/intangibles (common law)?
2. **Apply the predominant purpose test** for mixed goods-and-services contracts — which element predominates? (e.g., a contract to install custom software: is the software or the installation service the primary purpose?)
3. **Identify the key differences** that may affect the analysis:
   - Formation: UCC is more liberal (open terms, § 2-204; firm offers by merchants, § 2-205; battle of the forms, § 2-207)
   - Statute of frauds: UCC threshold is $500 (§ 2-201), with merchant confirmation rule
   - Warranties: UCC implies warranties of merchantability (§ 2-314) and fitness for a particular purpose (§ 2-315)
   - Perfect tender rule: UCC § 2-601 allows buyer to reject for any nonconformity (common law uses substantial performance)
   - Remedies: UCC provides specific buyer/seller remedy schemes
4. **Check for Article 2A** (leases of goods) or Article 9 (secured transactions) applicability
5. **State which law applies** and flag any provisions where the choice changes the outcome

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying UCC perfect tender rule to service contracts (service contracts use substantial performance)
- Applying common law mirror image rule to a sale of goods (UCC § 2-207 displaces it)
- Forgetting the merchant confirmation exception to the UCC statute of frauds
- Not addressing mixed transactions with the predominant purpose test
