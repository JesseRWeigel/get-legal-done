# Tort Analysis Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for tort law analysis.

## Protocol: Negligence Analysis

### When to Use
Evaluating claims where a party alleges injury due to another's failure to exercise reasonable care.

### Steps
1. **Establish duty** — did the defendant owe a duty of care to the plaintiff? Apply the general rule (duty to all foreseeable plaintiffs, Palsgraf) and check for special duty rules (landowner duties, negligent entrustment, undertaker doctrine, Tarasoff duty to warn)
2. **Determine the standard of care** — reasonable person standard (objective); adjust for professionals (standard of the profession), children (reasonable child of similar age/experience, except adult activities), and common carriers/innkeepers (heightened care)
3. **Assess breach** — did the defendant's conduct fall below the standard of care? Apply the Hand formula (B < P × L: burden of precaution vs probability and magnitude of harm), custom, and statutory standards (negligence per se if the statute was designed to protect this class of plaintiffs from this type of harm)
4. **Prove actual cause (cause-in-fact)** — but-for test (but for defendant's conduct, the injury would not have occurred); if multiple sufficient causes, use substantial factor test
5. **Prove proximate cause (legal cause)** — was the harm within the scope of the risk created by defendant's negligence? Check for superseding intervening causes (unforeseeable, independent) vs dependent intervening causes (foreseeable, do not break the chain)
6. **Establish damages** — economic (medical expenses, lost wages), non-economic (pain and suffering), and in rare cases punitive damages
7. **Assess affirmative defenses** — contributory negligence (complete bar in minority of jurisdictions), comparative negligence (pure vs modified/50% or 51% bar), assumption of risk (express or implied)
8. **State the conclusion** — identify the weakest element and the most likely defense

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Confusing actual cause (factual but-for) with proximate cause (legal/policy limitation)
- Applying contributory negligence in a comparative negligence jurisdiction or vice versa
- Treating negligence per se as conclusive rather than as creating a rebuttable presumption of breach
- Forgetting that duty is a question of law for the court, not a question of fact for the jury

## Protocol: Strict Liability Analysis

### When to Use
Evaluating liability without fault for abnormally dangerous activities or products liability.

### Steps
1. **Identify the theory** — abnormally dangerous activity (Restatement 2d § 520) or products liability (Restatement 3d)
2. **For abnormally dangerous activities**: apply the six Restatement factors — (a) high degree of risk, (b) likelihood of great harm, (c) inability to eliminate risk with reasonable care, (d) extent to which activity is not common, (e) inappropriateness of location, (f) value to the community vs danger
3. **Establish causation** — actual cause and proximate cause still required (strict liability removes the breach analysis, not the causation analysis)
4. **Confirm the harm is within the scope of the strict liability rule** — the plaintiff must suffer the type of harm that made the activity abnormally dangerous
5. **Assess defenses** — assumption of risk applies; contributory/comparative negligence applies in most jurisdictions for strict liability; abnormal use by plaintiff
6. **State the conclusion** — liability is/is not established; identify the controlling factor

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Assuming strict liability means absolute liability (defenses still apply; causation still required)
- Confusing strict liability for abnormally dangerous activities with products liability (different frameworks)
- Eliminating the causation requirement under strict liability
- Applying strict liability to activities that are common in the community (e.g., driving)

## Protocol: Products Liability Analysis

### When to Use
Evaluating claims against manufacturers, distributors, or retailers for harm caused by defective products.

### Steps
1. **Identify the defect type** — manufacturing defect (product departs from its intended design), design defect (reasonable alternative design available, per risk-utility test; or consumer expectations test), or warning/information defect (failure to warn of foreseeable risks)
2. **Choose the applicable test for design defect**:
   - **Risk-utility test** (Restatement 3d, majority) — plaintiff must show a reasonable alternative design (RAD) that would have reduced the risk at reasonable cost
   - **Consumer expectations test** (minority/California) — product failed to perform as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect
3. **Establish the chain of distribution** — manufacturer, distributor, and retailer are all potentially strictly liable; the product must have been defective when it left the defendant's control
4. **Prove causation** — the defect was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's injury
5. **Assess defenses** — product misuse (foreseeable misuse is not a defense), alteration after sale, comparative fault, learned intermediary doctrine (for prescription drugs/devices)
6. **Check for statutory limitations** — statute of repose (bars claims after a fixed period from sale), government contractor defense, preemption by federal safety standards
7. **State the conclusion** — identify the defect type, the strongest theory, and the most likely defense

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Requiring privity of contract for strict products liability claims (privity was eliminated by Greenman v. Yuba Power)
- Confusing the consumer expectations test with the risk-utility test (they can yield different results)
- Forgetting the reasonable alternative design requirement under the Restatement 3d risk-utility test
- Applying the learned intermediary doctrine outside the prescription drug/device context

## Protocol: Damages Calculation in Tort

### When to Use
Computing compensatory and punitive damages in tort claims.

### Steps
1. **Compute economic damages** — past and future medical expenses, lost earnings and earning capacity, property damage, and other out-of-pocket costs
2. **Compute non-economic damages** — pain and suffering (per diem or lump sum), emotional distress, loss of consortium, loss of enjoyment of life
3. **Apply the collateral source rule** — benefits received from third parties (insurance) do not reduce the defendant's liability (majority rule, though many states have modified this)
4. **Assess punitive damages** — available when defendant's conduct was willful, wanton, malicious, or reckless; must satisfy due process limits (BMW v. Gore: reprehensibility, ratio to compensatory damages, comparison to civil/criminal penalties; State Farm v. Campbell: single-digit ratio generally)
5. **Apply comparative fault reduction** — reduce damages by plaintiff's percentage of fault
6. **Consider statutory caps** — many states cap non-economic or punitive damages (e.g., medical malpractice caps)
7. **Discount future damages to present value** — use an appropriate discount rate for future economic losses

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Awarding punitive damages in ordinary negligence cases (requires aggravated misconduct)
- Ignoring statutory caps on damages that exist in the applicable jurisdiction
- Failing to discount future damages to present value
- Confusing the eggshell skull rule (take the plaintiff as you find them for extent of harm) with foreseeability of the type of harm
