# Regulatory and Administrative Law Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for regulatory and administrative law analysis.

## Protocol: Agency Authority Analysis (Post-Loper Bright)

### When to Use
Evaluating whether a federal agency's interpretation of a statute is legally valid after Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024) eliminated Chevron deference.

### Steps
1. **Identify the statutory provision** — pinpoint the exact statutory text the agency is interpreting
2. **Apply traditional tools of statutory construction** — text, structure, context, purpose, legislative history, canons of construction
3. **Determine if the statute is clear** — courts now decide the best reading of the statute de novo, without deference to the agency
4. **Assess whether Skidmore deference applies** — the agency's interpretation may still have persuasive weight based on: thoroughness of consideration, validity of reasoning, consistency with earlier/later pronouncements, and other persuasive factors
5. **Check for express delegation** — if Congress expressly delegated gap-filling authority (e.g., "the Secretary shall prescribe such regulations as may be necessary"), the agency has more latitude, but courts still review the boundaries
6. **Review the major questions doctrine** — if the regulation involves vast economic or political significance, courts require clear congressional authorization (West Virginia v. EPA, 2022)
7. **Evaluate reliance interests** — if the agency is changing a longstanding interpretation, assess whether regulated parties reasonably relied on the prior interpretation
8. **State the conclusion** — the regulation is valid/invalid under the best reading of the statute, with Skidmore weight assessed

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Still applying Chevron two-step analysis (Chevron was overruled by Loper Bright in 2024)
- Confusing Skidmore persuasive weight with Chevron binding deference
- Forgetting the major questions doctrine for regulations of vast significance
- Not specifying the exact statutory text being interpreted

## Protocol: APA Notice-and-Comment Rulemaking Analysis

### When to Use
Evaluating whether an agency followed proper procedure under the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. § 553.

### Steps
1. **Confirm the rule type** — is this a legislative rule (binding, requires notice-and-comment) or an interpretive rule/policy statement (exempt)?
2. **Check the notice** — was a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) published in the Federal Register? Did it include the terms or substance of the proposed rule, the legal authority, and an opportunity for comment?
3. **Evaluate the comment period** — was a meaningful opportunity to comment provided? (typically 30–60 days minimum; Executive Order 12866 recommends 60 days)
4. **Assess the agency's response** — did the agency respond to significant comments in the preamble to the final rule? (failure to respond = arbitrary and capricious under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm)
5. **Check the logical outgrowth test** — is the final rule a logical outgrowth of the proposed rule, or is it so different that a new notice was required?
6. **Verify the effective date** — legislative rules must have a 30-day delayed effective date unless good cause exists (§ 553(d))
7. **Check for exemptions** — military affairs, foreign affairs, agency management, and public property are exempt from § 553
8. **Assess arbitrary and capricious review** — under APA § 706(2)(A), did the agency examine relevant data, articulate a satisfactory explanation, and consider important aspects of the problem?

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Confusing legislative rules (binding, require notice-and-comment) with interpretive rules (exempt)
- Not applying the logical outgrowth test when the final rule differs from the proposed rule
- Forgetting the good cause exception to the 30-day delayed effective date requirement
- Treating the APA's arbitrary and capricious standard as equivalent to de novo review (it is deferential, though post-Loper Bright statutory interpretation is de novo)

## Protocol: Exhaustion and Ripeness of Administrative Claims

### When to Use
Determining whether a party can seek judicial review of an agency action or must first complete administrative proceedings.

### Steps
1. **Identify the statutory review scheme** — does the enabling statute specify procedures for administrative appeal before judicial review?
2. **Check for statutory exhaustion requirements** — if the statute requires exhaustion, it is jurisdictional and mandatory
3. **Apply judicially created exhaustion** — even without a statutory requirement, courts may require exhaustion as a prudential matter (but see Darby v. Cisneros: no judicial exhaustion requirement when the statute does not require it)
4. **Assess ripeness** — is the agency action final? (two-part test from Bennett v. Spear: (1) the action marks the consummation of the agency's decision-making process, and (2) the action is one by which rights or obligations have been determined or from which legal consequences will flow)
5. **Check for futility exception** — exhaustion may be excused if the administrative remedy would be futile or inadequate
6. **Evaluate hardship** — the fitness of the issues for judicial decision and the hardship to the parties of withholding review (Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner)
7. **State the conclusion** — the claim is/is not ripe for judicial review; exhaustion has/has not been satisfied

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Treating all exhaustion requirements as jurisdictional (only statutory exhaustion requirements are jurisdictional)
- Confusing ripeness with standing (ripeness = timing, standing = injury)
- Applying the futility exception too broadly without specific evidence that the administrative remedy is inadequate
- Forgetting that finality under the APA (§ 704) requires the action to be "final agency action"
