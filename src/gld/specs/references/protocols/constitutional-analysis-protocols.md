# Constitutional Analysis Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for constitutional law analysis.

## Protocol: Equal Protection Analysis

### When to Use
Claims under the Fourteenth Amendment Equal Protection Clause or Fifth Amendment (federal).

### Steps
1. **Identify the classification** — what characteristic is the basis for differential treatment?
2. **Determine the level of scrutiny**:
   - **Strict scrutiny**: race, national origin, religion, alienage (state), fundamental rights
     - Government must show: compelling interest + narrowly tailored
   - **Intermediate scrutiny**: sex/gender, legitimacy
     - Government must show: important interest + substantially related
   - **Rational basis**: everything else (age, disability, wealth, etc.)
     - Challenger must show: no legitimate interest OR not rationally related
   - **Rational basis with bite**: sometimes applied to animus-based classifications
3. **Research the specific classification** — how has the Court treated this type?
4. **Identify the comparator group** — who is being treated differently from whom?
5. **Analyze the government's justification** — what interest does the government assert?
6. **Check for recent developments** — equal protection doctrine continues to evolve

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying the wrong level of scrutiny (L007/L013)
- Conflating disparate treatment and disparate impact
- Missing the distinction between facial and as-applied challenges
- Citing pre-overruling affirmative action cases as good law (L003)

## Protocol: Due Process Analysis

### When to Use
Substantive or procedural due process claims under the Fifth or Fourteenth Amendment.

### Steps — Procedural Due Process
1. **Identify the protected interest** — life, liberty, or property
2. **Determine if a protected interest exists**:
   - Property: legitimate claim of entitlement (not just unilateral expectation)
   - Liberty: freedom from bodily restraint, reputation + tangible interest
3. **Apply the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test**:
   - Private interest affected
   - Risk of erroneous deprivation + value of additional safeguards
   - Government interest + fiscal/administrative burden
4. **Determine what process is due** — notice, hearing, right to counsel, etc.

### Steps — Substantive Due Process
1. **Identify the right at issue** — is it a fundamental right?
2. **If fundamental right** — apply strict scrutiny (Glucksberg framework):
   - Careful description of the asserted right
   - Deeply rooted in history and tradition?
   - If yes: compelling interest + narrowly tailored
3. **If not fundamental** — apply rational basis review
4. **Check for recent developments** — substantive due process is actively evolving

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Confusing substantive and procedural due process (L013)
- Applying outdated substantive due process frameworks (L003)
- Misidentifying what constitutes a "property" or "liberty" interest
- Missing the state-created danger exception to the no-duty-to-protect rule

## Protocol: First Amendment Analysis

### When to Use
Free speech, free exercise, establishment clause, freedom of association, freedom of the press.

### Steps — Free Speech
1. **Determine if government action is involved** — First Amendment applies to government, not private actors
2. **Identify the type of speech** — political, commercial, obscene, fighting words, true threats
3. **Determine if this is a content-based or content-neutral restriction**:
   - Content-based: strict scrutiny (Reed v. Town of Gilbert)
   - Content-neutral: intermediate scrutiny (Ward v. Rock Against Racism)
4. **Analyze the forum** — traditional public forum, designated public forum, limited public forum, nonpublic forum
5. **Check for applicable doctrines**:
   - Prior restraint (heavy presumption against)
   - Overbreadth and vagueness
   - Compelled speech
   - Government speech doctrine
6. **Research student speech, employee speech, prisoner speech** if applicable — special rules

### Steps — Free Exercise
1. **Determine if the law is neutral and generally applicable** (Employment Division v. Smith)
2. **If not neutral/generally applicable** — strict scrutiny (Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye)
3. **Check for RFRA/RLUIPA applicability** — statutory protections beyond constitutional minimum
4. **If RFRA applies**: substantial burden → compelling interest + least restrictive means
5. **Check for recent developments** — free exercise doctrine is actively evolving

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying outdated free speech tests (L003)
- Confusing content-based and content-neutral analysis (L013)
- Missing the government speech doctrine
- Applying pre-Fulton free exercise analysis (L003)
- Confusing First Amendment analysis with statutory religious liberty protections

## Protocol: Fourth Amendment Analysis

### When to Use
Search and seizure issues, warrant requirements, exclusionary rule.

### Steps
1. **Determine if there was a "search"** — reasonable expectation of privacy test (Katz) or property-based test (Jones)
2. **Determine if there was a "seizure"** — of person or property
3. **If search/seizure**: was there a warrant?
   - If yes: was the warrant supported by probable cause? Was it properly executed?
   - If no: does an exception apply?
4. **Warrant exceptions**: exigent circumstances, search incident to arrest, automobile exception, plain view, consent, inventory search, special needs, Terry stop
5. **Apply the exclusionary rule** — fruit of the poisonous tree, attenuation, independent source, inevitable discovery
6. **Check for standing** — was the defendant's own Fourth Amendment rights violated?
7. **Check for qualified immunity** if 1983 claim — was the law clearly established?

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Missing the Carpenter digital privacy framework for cell phone location data (L003)
- Applying outdated automobile search standards
- Confusing the standards for Terry stops and full searches
- Missing the good faith exception to the exclusionary rule
