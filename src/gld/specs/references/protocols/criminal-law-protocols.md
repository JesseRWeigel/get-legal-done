# Criminal Law Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for criminal law analysis.

## Protocol: Elements Analysis

### When to Use
Determining whether the elements of a criminal offense are satisfied based on the facts.

### Steps
1. **Identify the charged offense** — cite the specific statute or common law crime
2. **Parse the elements** — break the offense into its required components: actus reus (voluntary act or omission where duty exists), mens rea (mental state), attendant circumstances, causation (if a result crime), and result (if applicable)
3. **Analyze the actus reus** — was there a voluntary act (MPC § 2.01)? Involuntary acts (reflexes, convulsions, conduct during unconsciousness/sleep) do not satisfy the requirement. For omissions, identify the legal duty (statute, contract, relationship, voluntary assumption, creation of peril)
4. **Analyze the mens rea** — apply the MPC hierarchy: purposely (conscious object), knowingly (awareness of nature of conduct or practical certainty of result), recklessly (conscious disregard of substantial and unjustifiable risk), negligently (should have been aware of substantial and unjustifiable risk). If the statute uses common law terms (malice, willfully, specific intent, general intent), translate to the applicable meaning
5. **Apply the mens rea to each material element** — MPC § 2.02(4): if the statute prescribes one mental state without distinguishing among elements, it applies to all material elements
6. **Analyze causation (for result crimes)** — actual cause (but-for) AND proximate cause (not too remote or accidental). Check for intervening causes: coincidental (breaks the chain unless foreseeable) vs responsive (generally does not break the chain unless highly abnormal)
7. **State the conclusion** — all elements are/are not satisfied, identifying the weakest element

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Confusing MPC mental states (purposely, knowingly, recklessly, negligently) with common law terms (specific intent, general intent, malice)
- Applying strict liability mens rea without confirming the statute eliminates the mental state requirement
- Treating mere presence at the scene as sufficient actus reus (presence alone is not a voluntary act)
- Forgetting that omission liability requires a legal duty, not just a moral duty

## Protocol: Justification Defenses

### When to Use
Analyzing defenses that render otherwise criminal conduct lawful because it was justified under the circumstances.

### Steps
1. **Identify the claimed justification** — self-defense, defense of others, defense of property, necessity (choice of evils), or law enforcement authority
2. **For self-defense**: (a) Was there an honest and reasonable belief that force was necessary to prevent imminent unlawful force? (b) Was the force proportional? (c) Was deadly force used only against a threat of death, serious bodily harm, kidnapping, or sexual assault? (d) Was there a duty to retreat? (majority/MPC: retreat required if safe to do so before using deadly force, except in one's home/castle doctrine; minority/stand-your-ground: no duty to retreat)
3. **Check for the initial aggressor rule** — the initial aggressor generally cannot claim self-defense unless they withdrew and communicated withdrawal, or the victim escalated from non-deadly to deadly force
4. **For defense of others** — the defender steps into the shoes of the person defended; must reasonably believe that person would be entitled to use self-defense (MPC: reasonable belief standard; some jurisdictions: alter ego rule)
5. **For necessity/choice of evils** — (a) imminent threat of harm, (b) no lawful alternative, (c) the harm avoided outweighs the harm caused, (d) the defendant did not create the emergency. Necessity is generally not available for homicide
6. **Assess the burden of proof** — in most jurisdictions, the defendant bears the burden of production, and the prosecution bears the burden of persuasion (to disprove the defense beyond a reasonable doubt)
7. **State the conclusion** — the defense does/does not apply, identifying the element most likely to fail

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Confusing justification (conduct is right/permissible) with excuse (conduct is wrong but actor is not blameworthy)
- Applying stand-your-ground rules in duty-to-retreat jurisdictions
- Allowing necessity as a defense to intentional homicide (generally not permitted)
- Forgetting the initial aggressor limitation on self-defense

## Protocol: Excuse Defenses

### When to Use
Analyzing defenses where the conduct was wrongful but the defendant is not morally blameworthy.

### Steps
1. **Identify the claimed excuse** — insanity, intoxication, duress, infancy, entrapment, mistake of fact, mistake of law (rare)
2. **For insanity**: identify the applicable test — M'Naghten (did not know the nature/quality of the act or did not know it was wrong), MPC/ALI (lacked substantial capacity to appreciate criminality or conform conduct to law), irresistible impulse (unable to control actions), Durham (act was product of mental disease). Note: federal courts use a modified M'Naghten after the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984
3. **For voluntary intoxication**: defense only to specific intent crimes (negates the required mental state); not a defense to general intent, recklessness, or negligence crimes
4. **For involuntary intoxication**: treated like insanity — full defense if the intoxication rendered the defendant unable to understand the nature of the act or know right from wrong
5. **For duress**: (a) threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm, (b) to the defendant or a close family member, (c) that a reasonable person would have been unable to resist. Duress is generally not a defense to intentional homicide (but may reduce murder to manslaughter in some jurisdictions)
6. **For entrapment**: (a) government agent induced the crime, AND (b) the defendant was not predisposed to commit it (subjective test, federal/majority) OR the government conduct would have induced a law-abiding person (objective test, MPC/minority)
7. **State the conclusion** — the excuse does/does not apply, specifying the jurisdiction's test

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying the MPC insanity test in a jurisdiction that uses M'Naghten
- Allowing voluntary intoxication as a defense to general intent crimes
- Treating duress as a defense to murder (it is not in the vast majority of jurisdictions)
- Confusing subjective and objective entrapment tests

## Protocol: Sentencing Analysis

### When to Use
Analyzing sentencing issues including guidelines, constitutional constraints, and alternatives.

### Steps
1. **Identify the sentencing framework** — federal sentencing guidelines (advisory post-Booker), state determinate/indeterminate sentencing, mandatory minimums
2. **Calculate the guidelines range (federal)** — determine the base offense level, apply specific offense characteristics (adjustments for role, obstruction, acceptance of responsibility), determine the criminal history category, cross-reference on the sentencing table
3. **Apply statutory constraints** — mandatory minimums (override guidelines if higher), statutory maximums, safety valve provisions (18 U.S.C. § 3553(f) for certain drug offenses)
4. **Consider § 3553(a) factors** — nature and circumstances of the offense, history and characteristics of the defendant, need for the sentence to reflect seriousness/deterrence/incapacitation/rehabilitation, kinds of sentences available, guidelines range, policy statements, need to avoid unwarranted disparities, restitution
5. **Assess constitutional limitations** — Eighth Amendment (no cruel and unusual punishment, proportionality for non-capital cases under Solem v. Helm), Sixth Amendment (any fact increasing the sentence beyond the statutory maximum must be found by a jury beyond a reasonable doubt, Apprendi/Blakely/Booker)
6. **Consider alternatives** — probation, supervised release conditions, community service, restitution, diversion programs, restorative justice
7. **State the likely sentencing range** and identify factors that might justify a variance or departure

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Treating federal sentencing guidelines as mandatory (they are advisory after United States v. Booker, 2005)
- Ignoring Apprendi/Blakely requirements for judicial fact-finding that increases sentences
- Confusing departures (based on guidelines policy) with variances (based on § 3553(a) factors)
- Applying one state's sentencing framework to another state's criminal code
