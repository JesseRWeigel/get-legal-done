# Statutory Interpretation Protocols

> Step-by-step methodology guides for statutory analysis and interpretation.

## Protocol: Federal Statute Interpretation

### When to Use
Interpreting any federal statute — elements, scope, definitions, application.

### Steps
1. **Start with the statutory text** — always begin with the plain language
2. **Read the entire relevant section** — not just the clause at issue
3. **Check defined terms** — look for a definitions section (often at the beginning of the chapter)
4. **Apply canons of construction**:
   - Plain meaning rule
   - Whole act rule (interpret in context of entire statute)
   - Noscitur a sociis (known by its associates)
   - Ejusdem generis (of the same kind)
   - Expressio unius est exclusio alterius (expression of one excludes others)
   - Rule against surplusage (every word has meaning)
5. **Check for agency interpretation** — has the relevant agency interpreted this provision?
6. **Research legislative history** (if text is ambiguous):
   - Committee reports (most reliable)
   - Floor statements (less reliable)
   - Hearing testimony (least reliable)
7. **Check for subsequent amendments** — has Congress modified the provision?
8. **Search for judicial interpretations** — how have courts construed this language?
9. **Check for applicable regulations** — implementing regulations in the CFR

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Citing a superseded version of the statute (L006)
- Fabricating statutory provisions that don't exist (L004)
- Misapplying canons of construction
- Ignoring defined terms that change the plain meaning
- Relying on legislative history when the text is clear (textualist courts will reject this)

## Protocol: State Statute Interpretation

### When to Use
Interpreting state statutes — similar to federal but with state-specific canons.

### Steps
1. **Identify the current codification** — states recodify frequently
2. **Read the statutory text** — same plain meaning approach as federal
3. **Check for state-specific interpretive rules** — some states have statutory construction acts
4. **Research state court interpretations** — state supreme court is the final authority
5. **Check for uniform law origins** — many state statutes are based on uniform laws (UCC, Uniform Probate Code, etc.)
6. **If based on uniform law** — check the official comments and interpretations from other states
7. **Research the state's interpretive methodology** — textualist, purposivist, or mixed
8. **Check for preemption** — does federal law preempt the state statute?

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying the wrong state's version of a uniform law (L005)
- Citing outdated codification numbers (L006)
- Missing state constitutional constraints on statutory interpretation
- Applying federal interpretive canons to state statutes that follow different rules

## Protocol: Regulatory Interpretation

### When to Use
Interpreting federal regulations (CFR), agency guidance, and regulatory schemes.

### Steps
1. **Read the regulatory text** — start with the CFR provision
2. **Check the enabling statute** — what authority does the regulation implement?
3. **Read the preamble** — the Federal Register preamble to the final rule explains the agency's intent
4. **Check for agency guidance documents** — interpretive rules, policy statements, FAQ documents
5. **Determine deference standard**:
   - Legislative rules: historically Chevron deference (check current doctrine post-Loper Bright)
   - Interpretive rules: Skidmore deference (power to persuade)
   - Guidance documents: Auer/Kisor deference for agency interpretation of own regulations
6. **Search for judicial interpretations** — how have courts applied this regulation?
7. **Check for recent amendments** — regulations change via notice-and-comment rulemaking

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Applying outdated deference frameworks (L003) — this area is changing rapidly
- Citing superseded regulatory text (L006)
- Confusing proposed and final rules
- Treating agency guidance as having the force of law

## Protocol: Constitutional Interpretation

### When to Use
Interpreting constitutional provisions — federal or state constitution.

### Steps
1. **Read the constitutional text** — start with the actual language
2. **Identify the applicable interpretive framework**:
   - Originalism (original public meaning)
   - Living constitutionalism (evolving standards)
   - Structural arguments (structural inferences)
   - Precedent-based (stare decisis)
3. **Research the history of the provision** — Founding-era understanding, ratification debates
4. **Trace the doctrinal development** — how has the Supreme Court interpreted this provision?
5. **Identify the current test/framework** — most constitutional provisions have multi-part tests
6. **Check for recent doctrinal changes** — the current Court has been active in revising doctrine
7. **Apply the framework to the facts** — using the Court's own analytical structure
8. **Identify areas of doctrinal uncertainty** — where is the law unsettled?

### Common LLM Pitfalls
- Citing overruled constitutional precedent (L003) — particularly dangerous in current era of doctrinal change
- Conflating different levels of scrutiny (L013)
- Attributing concurrence reasoning to the majority (L010)
- Missing recent doctrinal shifts at the Supreme Court level
- Applying federal constitutional analysis to state constitutional claims (different frameworks)
